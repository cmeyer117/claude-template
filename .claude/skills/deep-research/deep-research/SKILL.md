# Deep Research Skill

The standard: every answer should be good enough that an expert in that field would respect it.
Not Wikipedia depth. Not surface summaries. Primary sources, respected practitioners, peer-reviewed
research, and proven frameworks — then synthesized clearly for application.

---

## Part 1: Depth Check Protocol

**Before researching any substantive question, always ask:**

```
📚 Research Depth Check

How deep do you want to go on this?

A) Quick hit — key facts, top takeaways, 2–3 minutes to read
B) Solid overview — mechanisms, evidence, practical application, 5–8 minutes
C) Full deep dive — everything: science, practitioner debate, edge cases, contrarian views
```

Exception: if the question is clearly casual or conversational, skip the check and answer directly.

---

## Part 2: Source Hierarchy

### How to weight sources:

1. **Peer-reviewed research** — highest weight for mechanism claims (how/why something works)
2. **Elite practitioners** — highest weight for application claims (what actually works in the real world)
3. **Institutional consensus** — strong weight for established principles (medicine, law, accounting standards)
4. **Respected books/authors** — strong weight for frameworks and mental models
5. **Industry leaders** — moderate weight, useful for current trends and emerging practices

### When sources conflict:
- Present BOTH the scientific position and the practitioner position
- Explain WHY they differ (lab vs. real world, study population, time horizon, etc.)
- Do NOT pick a side unless the evidence is overwhelmingly one-directional
- Let the user decide which to apply to their situation

## Part 4: Research Response Format

### Quick Hit (Option A):
- 3–5 bullet points
- One key source cited per major claim
- Bottom line up front

### Solid Overview (Option B):
- Mechanism explained (the why)
- Evidence summary (what the research says)
- Practitioner application (what the best in the field actually do)
- Conflicting views flagged if they exist
- 2–3 primary sources cited

### Full Deep Dive (Option C):
- Full mechanism breakdown
- Research consensus + outlier studies
- Elite practitioner methods with names
- Contrarian or emerging views
- Practical application to user's specific context
- Source list at the end
- Flag anything still actively debated

---

## Part 5: Conflict Presentation Format

When science and practitioners disagree, use this format:

```
⚖️ Conflicting Views on [Topic]

📖 What the research says:
[Scientific position + key study/researcher]

🏆 What top practitioners say:
[Practitioner position + who says it]

Why they differ:
[Explanation — lab conditions vs. real world, study population, time horizon, etc.]

Bottom line:
[Present both options, let user decide based on their context]
```

---

## Part 6: Citing Sources

- Always name the source, author, or institution behind a claim
- For practitioner knowledge: "According to Hany Rambod's FST-7 framework..."
- For research: "A 2019 meta-analysis by Schoenfeld et al. found..."
- For institutional: "Per AICPA standards..." / "Under IRC Section..."
- Never cite vaguely — "studies show" is not acceptable

---

## Part 7: Unknown Domain Protocol

When asked about a domain not explicitly covered in this skill:

1. **Identify the authority landscape first** — who are the top researchers, practitioners, institutions, and books in this field? State them before answering.
2. **Apply the same source hierarchy** — peer-reviewed → elite practitioners → institutional consensus → respected books
3. **Never fake authority** — if a domain is genuinely niche or emerging with no clear consensus, say so. Present the best available sources and flag the uncertainty.
4. **Pattern match to closest known domain** — e.g. sports nutrition question outside bodybuilding → apply the same researchers (Schoenfeld, Norton, Aragon) where applicable

### Format for unknown domains:
```
🔍 Authority sources for [domain]:
- Research: [who/where]
- Practitioners: [who]
- Institutions: [which]
- Key books/frameworks: [what]

Now diving in...
```
