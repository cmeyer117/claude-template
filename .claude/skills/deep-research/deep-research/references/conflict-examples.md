# Conflict Presentation Examples

Calibration reference for how to present conflicting information across domains.

---

## Example 1: Training Frequency (Bodybuilding)

⚖️ Conflicting Views on Training Frequency for Hypertrophy

📖 What the research says:
Meta-analyses by Schoenfeld et al. (2016) suggest training each muscle group 2x per week
produces superior hypertrophy vs. 1x per week when volume is equated. The mechanism:
muscle protein synthesis (MPS) spikes ~24–48 hours post-training, so more frequent
stimulation may capture more total MPS events.

🏆 What top practitioners say:
Hany Rambod's FST-7 system and many classic bodybuilding coaches still use high-volume,
lower-frequency bro splits (chest Monday, back Tuesday, etc.) with great success at
the pro level. Phil Heath, Jay Cutler, and many Mr. Olympia competitors trained this way.

Why they differ:
Study populations are typically natural, intermediate lifters — not enhanced, advanced
bodybuilders with years of adaptation. Recovery capacity, drug use, and individual
muscle fiber composition change the optimal frequency equation significantly at elite levels.

Bottom line:
If you're natural and intermediate → frequency evidence is strong, 2x/week per muscle.
If you're advanced with enhanced recovery → high-volume lower-frequency splits may work
better. Your coach's read on your recovery is more reliable than a general study here.

## Example 2: Carbohydrate Timing (Nutrition)

⚖️ Conflicting Views on Post-Workout Carb Timing

📖 What the research says:
Aragon & Schoenfeld (2013) showed the "anabolic window" is much wider than previously
thought — total daily protein and carb intake matter more than precise timing for most
people. The urgency of post-workout nutrition is largely overstated.

🏆 What top practitioners say:
Elite prep coaches like Chris Aceto still prioritize intra and post-workout nutrition
with fast-digesting carbs, particularly during contest prep when every variable matters.
Practical experience suggests it makes a difference at the margins.

Why they differ:
Research studies measure average effects across populations. At elite bodybuilding level,
you're optimizing at the margins — where 5% differences matter. General population
studies may not capture these marginal gains.

Bottom line:
For most training goals — total intake wins, timing is secondary.
For contest prep at a competitive level — timing the details may provide marginal edge
worth optimizing. Apply your coach's protocol.

## Example 3: Debt Payoff vs. Investing (Personal Finance)

⚖️ Conflicting Views on Paying Off Debt vs. Investing

📖 What the research says:
Standard financial theory: if debt interest rate < expected investment return (historically
~7–10% for index funds), mathematically you should invest. Paying off 4% mortgage early
while the market returns 10% is a net negative.

🏆 What top practitioners say:
Dave Ramsey and behavioral finance experts like Morgan Housel argue that the psychological
relief of being debt-free produces better long-term financial behavior — people who are
debt-free save and invest more consistently than those optimizing mathematically.

Why they differ:
The math assumes rational behavior. Behavioral finance shows humans are not rational
with money — peace of mind and reduced stress from zero debt often leads to better
real-world outcomes even if the math favors investing.

Bottom line:
High-interest debt (>7%) — pay it off, math and psychology both agree.
Low-interest debt (<4%) — math says invest, but consider your own psychology and
risk tolerance. No universally right answer.
